# Pedagogical / Structural Approaches Worth Adapting

Each item is an abstract method observed in the structure, re-expressed in our own words, with a rationale. None reproduces the source's wording, sequencing prose, examples, or exercises.

- **Split the product into a linear spine plus orbiting single-topic mini-courses.** A three-stage beginner→intermediate→advanced backbone is surrounded by standalone modules (counters, keigo, onomatopoeia, thematic vocab, reading). *Why it works:* learners get one clear path but can also binge a narrow weakness on demand, which raises perceived value and retention without bloating the main track.

- **Onboard one script at a time, with kana fully ahead of grammar.** Hiragana gets its own multi-lesson runway before any sentence work, and katakana is deliberately introduced later, after the learner already has spoken/grammatical footing. *Why it works:* it prevents the classic dual-script overwhelm and lets katakana ride on patterns the learner already trusts.

- **Interleave kanji with grammar instead of front-loading or fully deferring it.** Kanji starts a few modules in and then reappears in nearly every later module alongside new grammar. *Why it works:* spaced, little-and-often kanji exposure beats a single early wall or a late cram, and tying it to grammar the learner is meeting anyway keeps it contextual.

- **Run kanji as a parallel, drill-shaped track with its own fixed cadence.** A separate course delivers kanji in regular small batches (a near-constant lessons-per-module rhythm) with downloadable practice sheets. *Why it works:* predictable batch size sets a sustainable daily habit, and tangible worksheets convert passive recognition into active production.

- **Teach the polite verb register first as the default, then branch to plain/casual.** The spine establishes ます-form before exposing casual forms (and casual register gets its own supplement). *Why it works:* gives beginners a socially safe default they can use immediately, deferring register-switching complexity until they can handle it.

- **Lead with usable phrases before deep grammar.** Greetings, the copula, and simple questions appear very early so learners can produce real sentences before they understand the full machinery. *Why it works:* early wins and immediate communicative payoff are the strongest drivers of beginner motivation.

- **Offer a dedicated "fast start" survival course separate from the thorough spine.** A compact phrase-first course (survival expressions, numbers, dates, basic verbs) runs in parallel for learners who want to speak now. *Why it works:* serves the impatient/traveler segment without compromising the depth of the main syllabus — two audiences, one library.

- **Make the intermediate and advanced stages comprehension-driven.** Past the beginner wall, content shifts from discrete grammar lessons to graded reading and listening with grammar embedded in context. *Why it works:* high-volume comprehensible input is how grammar actually consolidates; it also mirrors real exposure better than endless isolated drills.

- **Build a graded reading library with full per-text audio.** The reading collection is the single most audio-dense part of the whole product. *Why it works:* pairing text with native audio trains decoding and listening together and gives learners reps they can do passively (commute, chores).

- **Give the counter system its own deep, family-by-family course.** Rather than one lesson, counters get dozens of focused units. *Why it works:* counters are a notorious long-tail pain point; isolating them lets learners master a high-friction area that most courses gloss over.

- **Carve out keigo, onomatopoeia, and manga/casual register as explicit standalone courses.** Topics most syllabi bury or skip are promoted to first-class mini-courses. *Why it works:* these are exactly the "I can read grammar but can't follow real Japanese" gaps; addressing them directly is a strong differentiator.

- **Keep every content unit small and uniformly shaped (theory body + media assets).** Each lesson is one focused page plus its own audio/video. *Why it works:* uniform, bite-sized units are easy to schedule, easy to resume, and easy to spaced-repeat.

- **Saturate the beginner stage with audio.** The foundation course carries by far the most audio clips per lesson. *Why it works:* listening and pronunciation are hardest to bootstrap at zero, so concentrating audio where the learner is most fragile pays the highest return.
