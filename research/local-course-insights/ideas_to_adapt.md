# Pedagogical / Structural Approaches Worth Adapting

Each item is an abstract method observed in the structure, re-expressed in our own words, with a one-line rationale. None reproduces the source's wording, sequencing prose, examples, or exercises. Items marked **(NEW L+)** were surfaced only on the deeper pass.

## Product architecture

- **Split the product into a linear spine plus orbiting single-topic mini-courses.** A three-stage beginner→intermediate→advanced backbone is surrounded by standalone modules (kanji, counters, keigo, onomatopoeia, thematic vocab, reading, manga). *Why:* one clear path, but learners can binge a narrow weakness on demand — higher perceived value without bloating the main track.

- **Ship a method/onboarding module *before* any language.** A pre-content module covers the study method and spaced repetition first. *Why:* frames the product as a habit/system, sets expectations, and primes the SRS behavior that drives retention.

- **(NEW L+) Run a second, function-first "express" beginner path in parallel to the thorough one.** The same N5 ground is delivered twice: once script-and-grammar-first (deep), once communicative-task-first (fast, PDF-backed). *Why:* serves the traveler/impatient segment and the thorough learner from one library without compromising either.

- **(NEW L+) Use a near-rigid per-module template at the intermediate/advanced stages: atomized grammar points → one applied situational reading → a guided "text-study" breakdown of that same text.** *Why:* the repeated ritual makes the course predictable to schedule and ensures every grammar point is immediately seen in authentic context and then re-explained — teach, apply, dissect.

## Scripts & kana

- **Onboard one script at a time, with kana fully ahead of grammar.** Hiragana gets its own multi-lesson runway; katakana is deliberately introduced later, after spoken/grammar footing exists. *Why:* avoids dual-script overwhelm; katakana rides on patterns the learner already trusts.

- **(NEW L+) Insert a grammar interlude *between* the two kana scripts.** A copula/greetings/questions module sits between hiragana and katakana rather than teaching both scripts back-to-back. *Why:* gives early communicative payoff and lets the second script feel like reinforcement, not a second wall.

- **(NEW L+) Teach kana one row per lesson, with voiced/semi-voiced, small-tsu, and contracted sounds as explicit separate units.** *Why:* tiny, uniform units are easy to spaced-repeat and prevent the "wall of 46 characters" effect.

## Kanji

- **Run kanji as a parallel, drill-shaped track with its own fixed cadence.** A separate course delivers kanji in regular small batches (a near-constant lessons-per-module rhythm) with practice material. *Why:* predictable batch size sets a sustainable daily habit.

- **(NEW L+) Use a 3-unit kanji batch template: new characters → vocabulary built from those characters → practice.** *Why:* immediately converts isolated glyphs into usable words and then into active practice, in one repeatable loop.

- **(NEW L+) Deliver kanji on two coordinated channels — a deep dedicated track plus a light recurring kanji-vocab lesson that opens later grammar modules.** *Why:* doubles exposure and ties new characters to the lexical field the learner is already studying, so kanji never feels disconnected from grammar.

## Verbs, grammar & register

- **Teach the polite verb register first as the default, then branch to plain/casual.** ます-form is established before casual forms. *Why:* a socially safe default beginners can use immediately; defers register-switching until they can handle it.

- **(NEW L+) Teach the polite-form conjugation as an explicit matrix (affirmative/past/negative/past-negative/volitional/desire) in one place.** *Why:* a single tabular mental model is faster to internalize than four scattered lessons.

- **Lead with usable phrases before deep grammar.** Greetings, copula, simple questions appear very early. *Why:* early wins and communicative payoff are the strongest beginner-motivation drivers.

- **(NEW L+) Cluster grammar by contrast set, not just by sequence.** Permission vs. non-necessity vs. prohibition are taught adjacently; the desire family is a consecutive suite; the conditional family (たら/ば/と/なら) is one module. *Why:* learners encode contrasts better when the alternatives are side-by-side.

- **(NEW L+) Co-locate the hard voice transforms (passive, causative, transitive/intransitive pairs) at the end of the foundation.** *Why:* grouping the conceptually related "who-does-what-to-whom" transforms lets them reinforce each other instead of appearing in isolation.

- **(NEW L+) Atomize advanced grammar to one construction per lesson, then bundle ~6–9 of them under a shared real-world reading.** *Why:* minimal per-lesson load plus a single context where several new patterns co-occur — spacing and integration at once.

## Reading, listening & input

- **Make the intermediate and advanced stages comprehension-driven.** Past the beginner wall, content pairs explicit grammar with graded reading and listening. *Why:* high-volume comprehensible input is how grammar actually consolidates.

- **Build a graded reading library with full per-text audio.** The reading collection is the most audio-dense part of the product. *Why:* pairing text with native audio trains decoding + listening together and enables passive reps (commute, chores).

- **(NEW L+) Grade the reading library by abstraction level, not just length: concrete-personal → expository-abstract → cultural-essay.** *Why:* difficulty scales along comprehension demand (genre/abstraction), which is a truer progression than word-count alone.

- **(NEW L+) Anchor each grammar module's reading in a concrete real-life scenario (clinic, bank, city hall, job interview, emergency call).** *Why:* situational anchoring makes grammar memorable and doubles as survival/functional training.

- **(NEW L+) Pair every reading with a dedicated breakdown/"text-study" unit.** *Why:* explaining the same text the learner just read closes the loop between exposure and understanding.

- **(NEW L+) Teach an authentic-media sentence-mining method (e.g., analyzing real comic sentences) as its own short track.** *Why:* bridges textbook Japanese to the messy real language learners actually want to consume, and teaches a transferable self-study skill.

## Breadth supplements

- **Give the counter system its own deep, family-by-family course (~32 counters).** *Why:* counters are a notorious long-tail pain point most courses gloss over; isolating them lets learners master a high-friction area.

- **Carve out keigo as a full, ordered sub-system course** (polite → respectful → humble → beautification → specific constructions → honorific vocab → over-honorific errors → reference table). *Why:* keigo is where "I know grammar but can't function professionally" learners stall; a systematic treatment is a strong differentiator.

- **(NEW L+) Explicitly teach common *errors* as content (e.g., over-honorific double-keigo to avoid).** *Why:* naming the trap is as valuable as teaching the rule; it prevents confident-but-wrong output.

- **Carve out onomatopoeia and casual/manga register as explicit standalone courses.** *Why:* exactly the "can read grammar but can't follow real Japanese" gaps; addressing them directly differentiates.

- **(NEW L+) Make thematic vocabulary modern and inclusive, organized by life-domain.** The theme set spans bureaucratic, medical, workplace, household, tech, and contemporary identity (LGBTQIA+) categories. *Why:* domain-organized, up-to-date vocabulary is both more useful and more marketable than dated generic word lists.

## Unit design & media

- **Keep every content unit small and uniformly shaped (one focused body + its own media).** *Why:* uniform bite-sized units are easy to schedule, resume, and spaced-repeat.

- **Saturate the beginner stage with audio.** The foundation course carries the most audio per lesson. *Why:* listening and pronunciation are hardest to bootstrap at zero; concentrate audio where the learner is most fragile.

- **(NEW L+) Match media type to learning goal per stage: per-sentence audio for the comprehension cores and reading library; single explainer videos for concept supplements; glyph/stroke images for the kanji track.** *Why:* media spend follows the skill being built, instead of one-size-fits-all production.

- **(NEW L+) Close the foundation with explicit learner-autonomy lessons** (collecting your own sentences, finding native content, dictionaries/tools, weaning off furigana). *Why:* deliberately scaffolds the hand-off to self-directed learning so graduates don't stall when the course ends.
