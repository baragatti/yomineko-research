# Pacing, Granularity, Grouping & Scaffolding Analysis

Method-level observations on *how* the course paces and shapes its material, derived from lesson counts, ordering, and per-lesson media metadata. No body text was read; all numbers are programmatic data points.

## 1. Lessons-per-module rhythm (data points)

| Track | Modules | Lessons/module (min–max) | Cadence shape |
|---|---|---|---|
| Beginner core (A) | 17 | 5–15 (avg ~10.5) | Irregular; swells where a script or form-cluster lands (15-lesson hiragana & desire/conjugation modules) |
| Intermediate core (B) | 13 | 2–10 (avg ~7.2) | Steady ~7–9, tapering at the end |
| Advanced core (C) | 12 | 5–10 (avg ~7.1) | Steady ~6–8 |
| Kanji track (K) | 16 | 3 (almost perfectly fixed) | Metronomic batch rhythm |
| Express (X) | 6 | 8–11 (avg ~9.7) | Even, fast |
| Counters (CT) | 1 | 32 in one flat module | One concept per unit |
| Keigo (KG) | 1 | 13 flat | One sub-system per unit |
| Onomatopoeia (ON) | 1 | 20 flat | One set per unit |
| Thematic vocab (V) | 1 | 36 flat | One theme per unit |
| Reading library (R) | 4 series | 6–13 | Graded by series |

**Takeaway:** the core spine uses *variable* module sizes (it expands where difficulty spikes), while every supplement uses a *flat, uniform* unit size. The kanji track is the most rigid (a 3-unit metronome) — deliberately habit-forming.

## 2. Where the course lingers vs. rushes

- **Lingers on scripts:** ~28 lessons across two modules just for hiragana + katakana (one kana-row per lesson, plus voiced/contracted/special sounds and two full reviews). Scripts get the slowest, most granular treatment in the whole product.
- **Lingers on the て-form complex:** the て-form and its derivatives (〜ている, 〜てください, 〜てもいい, 〜てしまう, 〜てから, benefactive 〜て-verbs, etc.) are spread across ~5 modules — the single most-revisited grammatical hub.
- **Lingers on conditionals:** an entire module clusters たら/ば/と/なら + obligation forms together rather than scattering them.
- **Lingers on counters:** 32 separate lessons for counters alone — far deeper than a typical syllabus, which usually gives counters one or two lessons.
- **Rushes practical time/date vocabulary:** calendar/time material is comparatively thin and arrives late, despite being high-utility survival content.
- **Rushes the advanced tail:** modules 11–12 of both intermediate and advanced cores shrink to 5 lessons, suggesting momentum tapers near each course's end.
- **Compresses the whole N5 band into a second, parallel track** (express): the same beginner ground that takes ~178 lessons in the core is re-delivered in ~58 task-first lessons for impatient learners.

## 3. Spacing of new items

- **Scripts are spaced, not dumped:** kana is introduced family-by-family with a grammar interlude (copula/greetings) deliberately inserted *between* hiragana and katakana, so the second script rides on already-trusted patterns.
- **Kanji is doubly spaced:** a dedicated 16-batch track plus a *recurring lighter* kanji-vocabulary lesson that opens nearly every beginner module from ~module 8 onward. New kanji therefore arrives in small, regular doses on two coordinated channels.
- **Grammar is atomized:** in the intermediate/advanced cores, almost every lesson introduces exactly one construction — maximal spacing, minimal per-lesson load.
- **No visible spiral review:** items appear once in sequence; there is no structural mechanism (re-test lesson, cumulative review module) that re-surfaces earlier grammar inside later lessons, aside from end-of-section reference tables. (See gaps_to_beat.md.)

## 4. Grouping logic (what is taught together)

- **Particles are clustered by function**, not introduced one-per-module: range particles (から/まで) together; means/accompaniment (で/と) together; object/also (を/も) together.
- **Permission/prohibition pairs** (〜てもいい / 〜なくてもいい / 〜ないでください) are taught adjacently as a contrast set.
- **Desire family** (ほしい / 〜たい / 〜てほしい / 〜ないでほしい) is taught as one consecutive suite.
- **Benefactive give/receive verbs** are grouped with their 〜て-forms in a single module.
- **Voice transforms** (passive, causative, transitive/intransitive pairs) are deliberately co-located at the end of the beginner course.
- **Each B/C module bundles ~5–9 thematically loose grammar points with one shared situational reading** that exercises several of them at once — grouping by *application context* rather than by grammatical family at the intermediate stage.
- **Kanji-vocabulary is grouped with the grammar module it opens**, tying new characters to the lexical field of nearby lessons.

## 5. Scaffolding patterns (method-level)

- **Intro → atomized practice → applied text → guided breakdown.** The dominant cadence, especially in B/C: teach one point, then several more, then drop them into a single narrated real-world text, then run a "text study" lesson that dissects that same text. Recognition (reading/listening) is scaffolded immediately into context.
- **Phrase-first onboarding.** Usable output (greetings, copula, simple questions) precedes the underlying grammar, front-loading communicative wins.
- **Polite-register-first.** The polite ます-form is established as the safe default before plain/casual forms appear, deferring register-switching complexity.
- **Recognition-before-production for kanji.** Kanji lessons lean on glyph imagery + reading practice (recognition), with production pushed to downloadable worksheets — a recognition-heavy balance.
- **Capstone reference artifacts.** Conjugation tables and word-class reviews are parked at the end of the relevant section as consolidation references rather than teaching units.
- **Autonomy hand-off.** The beginner course closes with "how to keep learning without us" lessons (sentence collection, finding native media, dictionaries, removing furigana) — explicitly scaffolding the transition to self-directed input.
- **Method module up front.** A pre-language module 0 teaches the study method and SRS before any Japanese, framing the whole product as a habit/system, not just content.

## 6. Media profile per stage (scaffolding via media)

| Stage | Audio density | Video | Images | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Beginner core | ~13 audio/lesson avg, ~76% of lessons | ~93% | rare | Saturate the fragile zero-stage with listening; explainer video almost everywhere |
| Intermediate core | high, 98% of lessons | ~85% | none | Per-sentence audio sustains listening as texts lengthen |
| Advanced core | highest, 100% of lessons | ~86% | none | Densest prose, fully narrated |
| Reading library | 100% audio | ~54% | none | Pure read-and-listen; the most listening-dense set in the product |
| Kanji track | none | ~68% | many (stroke/glyph) | Visual-recognition oriented |
| All other supplements | none | ~100% | rare | Single explainer video per topic; lightweight |

**Pattern:** audio is concentrated exactly where comprehension is built (the three cores + reading library); supplements are lean single-video units. Images appear only where the goal is visual recognition (kanji).

## 7. Net pacing characterization

The course is **front-heavy and fine-grained at the bottom** (kana and early grammar crawl), then shifts to a **rhythmic atomize-and-apply belt** for intermediate/advanced grammar, with **supplements running as flat, uniform, habit-shaped side-streams**. Its strongest pacing ideas are the metronomic kanji batches, the per-module grammar→reading→breakdown ritual, and the doubly-spaced kanji delivery. Its weakest pacing traits are the absence of spiral review, the late placement of high-utility practical vocabulary, and a difficulty step-up from the very granular beginner course into the denser grammar belt.
