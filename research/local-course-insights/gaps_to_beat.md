# Gaps / Weaknesses We Can Beat

Concept-level, specific, actionable. Each is a structural deficiency inferred from the topic map and metadata — not a quotation or critique of any specific wording. Items marked **(NEW L+)** were surfaced only on the deeper pass.

## Coverage gaps (missing concepts)

- **No pitch-accent treatment anywhere.** Across all ~621 lessons there is zero dedicated pitch-accent (高低アクセント) content. *Beat it:* make pitch a first-class topic from the start — mark accent on vocabulary, teach the basic patterns early, tie it into audio drills. A major, well-known differentiator.

- **Pronunciation is shallow and front-loaded only.** Phonetics appears once in early beginner material and is never revisited. *Beat it:* give pronunciation a recurring thread (mora timing, long vowels, devoicing, the ん allophones, intonation, pitch) that reappears across levels.

- **(NEW L+) No vowel-devoicing or sentence-intonation content.** Even the audio-dense cores never name devoicing or intonation patterns as teachable items. *Beat it:* teach devoicing and rising/falling intonation explicitly and reinforce them against the per-sentence audio.

- **No explicit JLPT / level scaffolding.** Nothing maps content to N5–N1 or signals where a learner stands. *Beat it:* tag every lesson with a level and a can-do statement so learners (and exam-takers) always know their position and what unlocks next.

- **No visible cultural / usage-note layer.** No lesson titles indicate culture notes, register nuance, or "when do natives actually say this." *Beat it:* attach short usage/culture/pragmatics notes to grammar and vocabulary so learners avoid textbook-correct-but-unnatural output.

- **(NEW L+) Counters are taught in isolation, not as a navigable system.** 32 counters get one flat lesson each with no rule-of-thumb framework, no "which counter for which noun" decision aid, and no shared exception patterns (e.g., the irregular readings of 本/匹/杯). *Beat it:* add a unifying counter-selection framework and a searchable counter↔noun map on top of the per-counter drills.

- **(NEW L+) Onomatopoeia is delivered as opaque numbered sets with no semantic grouping in the titles.** Lessons are just "Lesson 01…20," giving learners no map of which sound-symbolic field they're studying. *Beat it:* organize onomatopoeia by sense/domain (sound vs. state, emotion, texture, movement) with searchable tags.

## Sequencing problems

- **Katakana is delayed quite far behind hiragana.** Learners go a long stretch unable to read loanwords, menus, and brand text everywhere in real life. *Beat it:* introduce katakana much closer to hiragana — loanwords are high-utility and high-motivation early.

- **Everyday practical vocabulary (dates / time / calendar) is parked late.** Core survival material is taught well after it would be useful. *Beat it:* move time/date/scheduling vocabulary to the early-beginner band.

- **Adjectives arrive relatively late, after substantial verb material.** Delays the ability to describe things — a very common beginner need. *Beat it:* introduce basic i/na adjectives earlier so learners can describe and give opinions sooner.

- **The casual / plain form is taught after polite and partly off-loaded to supplements.** A spine-only learner over-indexes on polite speech. *Beat it:* teach plain↔polite as a paired system inside the core track from the moment verbs appear.

- **(NEW L+) Comparison and conditionals land near the very end of the foundation.** High-frequency functions (comparing options, "if/when") are deferred to the final beginner modules. *Beat it:* introduce a simple comparison pattern and at least one conditional earlier, then deepen later.

## Depth / clarity / pacing problems

- **(NEW L+) No spiral review across the whole product.** Grammar points are introduced once in sequence; aside from end-of-section reference tables there is no mechanism re-surfacing earlier grammar inside later lessons. *Beat it:* implement deliberate spaced re-exposure so earlier grammar reappears in later contexts and assessments.

- **(NEW L+) The intermediate/advanced cores are a long flat list of ~130 atomized grammar points with no inter-point map.** Each construction is its own lesson but there's no clustering by meaning (e.g., all the "probability/evidentiality" forms, all the "concession" forms shown together) and no disambiguation between near-synonyms. *Beat it:* add a grammar atlas that groups near-synonymous patterns and contrasts their nuance side-by-side.

- **(NEW L+) The applied readings are fixed and non-adaptive.** Each B/C module's situational text is one static narrated passage with a single breakdown — no inline lookups, no difficulty variants, no comprehension checks. *Beat it:* add tap-to-gloss, furigana toggles, multiple difficulty versions, and comprehension questions per text.

- **A hard difficulty cliff between the very granular beginner course and the denser grammar belt.** The foundation is extremely fine-grained (~178 lessons); the next stage jumps to atomized grammar packed around dense narrated texts. *Beat it:* add a graded bridge tier with scaffolded texts and just-in-time grammar so the transition is gradual.

- **The beginner course may be slow to a fault for the N5 band.** ~178 lessons before the intermediate stage risks fatigue and drop-off. *Beat it:* offer an accelerated/placement path so faster or returning learners can compress the foundation without re-doing kana from zero. (The source's own "express" track hints at this need but it's a separate product, not a placement path.)

- **Fragmented, 11-product structure with no unified progress spine.** Breadth without a single map showing how supplements (kanji, counters, keigo, reading) slot into the main journey. *Beat it:* provide one integrated roadmap/skill tree that schedules the supplements at the right moment in the main progression.

- **(NEW L+) Two parallel beginner paths (thorough vs. express) with no defined relationship.** A learner can't tell whether to do one, the other, or both, or how they map onto each other. *Beat it:* unify them into a single adaptive path with a "fast track / full track" toggle and shared progress.

- **Kanji practice depends on downloadable worksheets rather than interactive review.** Reinforcement is files to print/fill, not in-app spaced repetition. *Beat it:* build interactive, SRS-backed kanji review (recognition + production) instead of static downloads.

- **(NEW L+) Kanji is recognition-skewed.** The kanji track leans on glyph imagery and reading practice with production pushed to offline sheets. *Beat it:* balance recognition and active recall/writing with stroke-order practice and production prompts in-app.

- **Reading is presented as fixed graded texts without adaptive support.** No inline lookups, tap-to-gloss, or difficulty adaptation. *Beat it:* add tap-to-define, furigana toggles, and comprehension checks so reading scales to the individual learner.

- **(NEW L+) No assessment, placement, or progress-measurement layer is visible anywhere.** No quizzes, no level tests, no can-do checkpoints in the structure. *Beat it:* add formative quizzes, a placement test, and milestone assessments tied to level/can-do tags.

- **(NEW L+) Method depends on an external SRS app rather than an integrated one.** The course teaches using a third-party spaced-repetition tool. *Beat it:* build SRS natively into the product so review, content, and progress live in one place.

- **(NEW L+) Course/module names carry little information ("Topic N").** The core courses use generic placeholder module titles, so learners can't navigate by concept. *Beat it:* give every module a descriptive, concept-named title and a contents preview so learners can target what they need.
