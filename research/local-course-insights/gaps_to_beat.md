# Gaps / Weaknesses We Can Beat

Concept-level, specific, actionable. Each is a structural deficiency inferred from the topic map and metadata — not a quotation or critique of any specific wording.

## Coverage gaps

- **No pitch-accent treatment anywhere.** Across all 621 lessons there is zero dedicated pitch-accent (高低アクセント) content. *Beat it:* make pitch a first-class topic from the start — mark accent on vocabulary, teach the basic patterns early, and tie it into the audio drills. This is a major, well-known differentiator.

- **Pronunciation is shallow and only at the very beginning.** Phonetics appears once in early beginner material and is not revisited. *Beat it:* give pronunciation a recurring thread (mora timing, long vowels, devoicing, the ん allophones, pitch) that reappears across levels instead of a one-time intro.

- **No explicit JLPT / level scaffolding.** Nothing in the structure maps content to N5–N1 or signals where a learner stands. *Beat it:* tag every lesson with a level and a can-do statement so learners (and exam-takers) always know their position and what unlocks next.

- **No visible cultural / usage-note layer.** No lesson titles indicate culture notes, register nuance, or "when do natives actually say this" context. *Beat it:* attach short usage/culture/pragmatics notes to grammar and vocabulary so learners avoid textbook-correct-but-unnatural output.

## Sequencing problems

- **Katakana is delayed quite far behind hiragana.** Learners go a long stretch unable to read loanwords, menus, and brand/product text that are everywhere in real life. *Beat it:* introduce katakana much closer to hiragana (loanwords are high-utility and high-motivation early on).

- **Everyday practical vocabulary (dates / time / calendar) is parked at the very end of the beginner course.** This is core survival material taught as a capstone instead of early. *Beat it:* move time/date/scheduling vocabulary to the early-beginner band where it is immediately useful.

- **Adjectives arrive relatively late, after substantial verb material.** This delays the ability to describe things — a very common beginner need. *Beat it:* introduce basic i/na adjectives earlier so learners can describe and express opinions sooner.

- **The casual / plain form is split off into separate products instead of integrated.** Plain form and colloquial register live in supplements rather than the main spine, so a spine-only learner over-indexes on polite speech. *Beat it:* teach plain↔polite as a paired system inside the core track once verbs are introduced.

## Depth / clarity / pacing problems

- **Intermediate and advanced stages have almost no explicit grammar scaffolding.** Past the beginner wall, grammar is embedded inside reading texts with very few labeled grammar points, so learners cannot easily look up or target a specific structure. *Beat it:* keep the input-rich reading but layer an explicit, searchable grammar index over it (each text annotated with the grammar points it exercises).

- **A hard difficulty cliff between the beginner course and the reading-only intermediate course.** The beginner stage is extremely fine-grained (178 lessons); the next stage abruptly switches to comprehension-driven texts with sparse explicit teaching. *Beat it:* add a graded bridge tier with scaffolded texts and just-in-time grammar so the transition is gradual.

- **The beginner course may be slow to a fault — heavy lesson count for the N5 band.** 178 lessons before the intermediate stage risks fatigue and drop-off. *Beat it:* offer an accelerated/placement path so faster or returning learners can compress the foundation without re-doing kana from zero.

- **Fragmented, multi-product structure with no unified progress spine.** Eleven separate courses give breadth but no single map showing how supplements (counters, keigo, kanji, reading) slot into the main journey. *Beat it:* provide one integrated roadmap/skill tree that schedules the supplements at the right moment in the main progression.

- **Kanji practice depends on downloadable worksheets rather than interactive review.** Kanji reinforcement is delivered as files to print/fill rather than in-app spaced repetition. *Beat it:* build interactive, SRS-backed kanji review (recognition + production) instead of static downloads.

- **Reading is presented as fixed graded texts without adaptive support.** No indication of inline lookups, tap-to-gloss, or difficulty adaptation around the texts. *Beat it:* add tap-to-define, furigana toggles, and comprehension checks so reading scales to the individual learner.

- **Grammar points are not cross-linked or revisited systematically.** The structure shows topics introduced once in sequence with no visible spiral-review mechanism. *Beat it:* implement deliberate spaced re-exposure so earlier grammar resurfaces inside later lessons.
