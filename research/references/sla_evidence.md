# SLA Evidence Base for Course Pedagogy (zero → N5 → N4)

This note distills the empirical second-language-acquisition (SLA) and cognitive-psychology literature into concrete design rules for our Brazilian-Portuguese → Japanese course. For each topic it states **the finding**, **strength of evidence**, and a **design rule** we can act on, ending with an explicit implication.

The overall picture: the strongest, most replicated effects come from **cognitive-psychology mechanisms** (retrieval practice, spacing, dual coding) rather than from the classic SLA *hypotheses* (Krashen's input, Swain's output), which are theoretically influential but empirically softer. Our pedagogy should be **engineered on the strong-evidence mechanisms** and use the SLA hypotheses as *design heuristics*, not as load-bearing claims.

---

## 1. Comprehensible Input & the i+1 Hypothesis (Krashen, and critiques)

**The claim.** Krashen's Input Hypothesis holds that we acquire language in one way only — by understanding messages slightly beyond our current level ("i+1"). Acquisition (subconscious) is sharply separated from learning (conscious rule-knowledge), and an "affective filter" (anxiety, low motivation) can block intake. Output and grammar instruction are, on this view, largely peripheral.

**Strength of evidence: weak as a falsifiable theory; partially true as a heuristic.** The central, durable insight — that learners need large volumes of understandable input — is uncontroversial and well-supported. But the *specific* hypothesis is widely judged **untestable and underspecified**: "i" (the learner's current level) and "+1" (the next increment) are never operationally defined, so the formula cannot be measured or falsified ([Input hypothesis — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Input_hypothesis); [Lai 2019, A Critical Evaluation of Krashen's Monitor Model](https://www.academypublication.com/issues2/tpls/vol09/11/13.pdf)). Critics (Gregg, McLaughlin, White and others, summarized in those critiques) argue the acquisition/learning split assumes a separation never empirically demonstrated, and that the model ignores the demonstrated role of output and interaction. A 2025 neuro-ecological critique argues language development is active, embodied, and feedback-driven, so a "one-size-fits-all i+1" misses individual learner pathways ([Frontiers in Psychology 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1636777/full)). Net: keep the *spirit* (lots of comprehensible input, low anxiety), discard reliance on the *literal mechanism*.

**Design rule.** Make every lesson's core content **comprehensible at roughly 90–98% known material** (see §4 for the coverage rationale), introduce a small, controlled slice of new items per lesson, and keep affect low (clear scaffolding, no public failure). Do **not** rely on input alone — pair it with retrieval and output (§2, §5).

**Implication for our course:** Input volume is necessary but not sufficient; we anchor design on the measurable mechanisms below and treat "comprehensible input at the learner's edge" as a content-difficulty constraint (~95% known), not as our theory of how acquisition happens.

## 2. The Testing Effect / Retrieval Practice (Roediger & Karpicke et al.)

**The finding.** Actively recalling information produces far better long-term retention than re-studying it — the "testing effect." Roediger & Karpicke (2006) showed a single test can beat repeated re-reading for delayed retention, with practice-tested learners remembering substantially more after a delay than re-readers (the classic figure is roughly a 50% retention advantage at one week) ([Roediger & Karpicke 2006, Perspectives on Psychological Science, WashU PDF](http://psychnet.wustl.edu/memory/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/Roediger-Karpicke-2006_PPS.pdf)). The landmark Karpicke & Roediger (2008) *Science* study taught foreign-language word pairs and then dropped items from either further study or further testing once first recalled: **dropping items from study barely hurt delayed recall, but dropping them from testing collapsed it.** Repeated *retrieval*, not repeated *study*, drove retention — and learners' confidence judgments were uncorrelated with actual performance (they could not tell which strategy worked) ([Karpicke & Roediger 2008, Science, MIT-hosted PDF](https://web.mit.edu/jbelcher/www/learner/retrieval.pdf)).

**Strength of evidence: very strong.** One of the most replicated effects in learning science, confirmed in classroom meta-analyses (e.g., the testing effect generalizes to authentic instruction; [Schwieren, Barenberg & Dutke 2017, *Psychology Learning & Teaching* meta-analysis](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1475725717695149)).

**Design rule.** Build the course so **recall is the default activity, not re-reading.** Every item (vocab, kanji, grammar pattern) must be repeatedly *retrieved* — produce the word/reading from a cue, not just re-recognize it. Critically, **do not drop an item from review just because the learner got it right once**; keep testing it across spaced sessions. Because metacognition is unreliable, the system (SRS) — not the learner's feeling of "I know this" — must schedule reviews.

**Implication for our course:** Retrieval is the engine. Lessons present, but reviews *test*. Minimum target: every new item is retrieved (not merely re-shown) at least 3–4 times across the first week, with the schedule machine-driven (§3).

## 3. The Spacing Effect & Expanding Retrieval (informs our SRS)

**The finding.** Distributing study across time beats massing it ("cramming"), and the optimal gap scales with how long you need to remember. Cepeda et al. (2006) meta-analyzed 317 experiments (839 comparisons): spaced practice reliably beat massed, and the best inter-study interval grows with the target retention interval ([Cepeda, Pashler, Vul, Wixted & Rohrer 2006, *Psychological Bulletin*, author abstract page](https://www.yorku.ca/ncepeda/publications/CPVWR2006.html)). Cepeda et al. (2008) trained 1,350+ people and tested up to a year later, mapping a "temporal ridgeline": **the optimal gap is roughly 10–20% of the desired retention interval** — about 20–40% of a one-week target, falling to ~5–10% for a one-year target ([Cepeda et al. 2008, *Psychological Science*, UCSD PDF](https://laplab.ucsd.edu/articles/Cepeda%20et%20al%202008_psychsci.pdf); [author summary](https://www.yorku.ca/ncepeda/publications/CVRWP2008.html)). On **expanding vs. equal-interval** schedules: the popular belief that ever-expanding gaps are uniquely optimal is **not well supported.** Karpicke & Roediger (2007) found expanding schedules win on *immediate* tests but equal-interval (or even a well-placed first retrieval) wins for *delayed* retention; **the placement of the first successful retrieval matters more than the expansion pattern** ([Karpicke & Roediger 2007, *JEP:LMC*, Purdue PDF](https://learninglab.psych.purdue.edu/downloads/2007/2007_Karpicke_Roediger_JEPLMC.pdf)).

**Strength of evidence: very strong for spacing; nuanced for "expanding."** Spacing is among the most robust findings in psychology. The "expanding-is-best" assumption common in flashcard apps is weakly supported — absolute spacing and a successful (not too-easy, not too-failed) first retrieval matter more than the exact curve.

**Design rule.** Use an SRS with **expanding-ish intervals as a reasonable default but tuned to a realistic retention horizon** (for N5→N4 we want months-to-exam retention, so gaps on the order of a few days early, then weeks). Don't fetishize aggressive expansion — guarantee a **prompt, successful first review** within ~1 day of introduction, then space. Set initial intervals so the learner reliably *succeeds* on the first review (failed retrieval wastes the effect).

**Implication for our course:** Concrete schedule for new items: introduce → review ~Day 1 → ~Day 3 → ~Day 7 → ~Day 16 → ~Day 35+ (each gap roughly 10–20% of the next horizon). Anki-style scheduling is fine; the design priority is *early successful retrieval*, not exotic expansion math.

## 4. Frequency-Based Vocabulary & Lexical Coverage Thresholds (Japanese specifics)

**The finding.** Comprehension depends on **lexical coverage** — the % of running words a reader knows. Two thresholds recur: **95% = minimally acceptable** (reader follows main ideas but misses nuance, ~1 unknown word per 20) and **98% = adequate for unassisted comprehension** (~1 unknown per 50) ([Hu & Nation 2000, *Reading in a Foreign Language*]; Laufer 1989 lexical-threshold hypothesis; both synthesized in [vocabulary-size-coverage research note (tkgally/je-dict-1)](https://github.com/tkgally/je-dict-1/blob/main/planning/wiki/research/vocabulary-size-coverage.md)). For **English**, Nation (2006) found ~8,000–9,000 word families needed for 98% of written text; the curve is steep then flat — the first ~2,000 families cover ~80–85% of text, after which each 1,000 adds far less ([Nation 2006, *Canadian Modern Language Review*](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/studies-in-second-language-acquisition); coverage figures in the je-dict-1 note above). **Japanese-specific caveats:** (a) the "word family" unit does not transfer cleanly; (b) Sino-Japanese compounding is highly productive (knowing 経済 + 学 gives a shot at 経済学), so coverage gained per root morpheme can be *higher* than English; (c) script knowledge (kanji recognition) is a separate axis from form-meaning knowledge; (d) Sato (2014) found high-frequency words make up a *smaller* share of tokens in Japanese than English, so **Japanese may need somewhat more vocabulary for equivalent coverage**, and demand varies more by genre. Rough JLPT anchors widely cited: N5 ~800 words, N4 ~1,500, N3 ~3,750, N2 ~6,000, N1 ~10,000–12,000 — with N1 roughly comparable to the 8–9k English word families for 98% written coverage (all from the je-dict-1 synthesis citing Matsushita 2012, Tono/Yamazaki/Maekawa 2013, Sato 2014).

**Strength of evidence: strong for the threshold framework; moderate for exact Japanese numbers.** The 95/98% thresholds are field-standard, though Kremmel & Schmitt (2023) replicated Hu & Nation and found 98% is not a guaranteed comprehension floor for all populations (background knowledge and reading skill also matter). Japanese coverage numbers are less established than English because there is no widely adopted Japanese equivalent of Nation's BNC word-family lists.

**Design rule.** Sequence vocabulary **by corpus frequency**, front-loading the high-yield core (the first ~1,000–2,000 items deliver the steepest coverage gains). For any reading/listening text we author, keep learner-known coverage at **≥95% (ideally ~98%) of tokens** so context can carry the rest. Exploit Sino-Japanese productivity: teach high-frequency kanji/morphemes as *combinable units* so coverage compounds (ties to §9). Track vocabulary as two linked axes — spoken form-meaning and kanji recognition.

**Implication for our course:** Target ~800 high-frequency words by end of N5 and ~1,500 by end of N4, ordered by frequency, and design every reading so the learner already knows ~95%+ of its words. Build the spaced-frequency list first; lessons draw from it in order.

## 5. The Output Hypothesis & Pushed Output for Speaking (Swain)

**The finding.** Swain (1980s, from Canadian French-immersion data where learners had excellent comprehension but weak production) argued that *producing* language does cognitive work input cannot: it forces learners to (1) **notice** gaps between what they want and can say, (2) **test hypotheses** about grammar and get feedback, and (3) **reflect metalinguistically** ([Comprehensible output — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comprehensible_output)). "Pushed output" = tasks that demand production stretched slightly beyond comfort.

**Strength of evidence: moderate and mixed.** There is no direct proof that output *alone* causes acquisition, and the construct is under-defined. But intervention studies show pushed output can improve **accuracy and fluency** to a statistically significant degree in specific contexts ([Nobuyoshi/Ellis-style pushed-output study, ERIC PDF](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1127288.pdf)). A real risk: pushing too hard raises anxiety and lowers motivation (relevant given Krashen's affective filter). The immersion-program origin is itself the strongest naturalistic evidence that comprehension-only input leaves a production gap.

**Design rule.** Include **production tasks in every lesson**, not just recognition. Make output *pushed but supported*: cue the learner to produce a target form (speak/type the sentence, transform a pattern, answer aloud), then give immediate corrective feedback so the noticing/hypothesis-testing loop closes. Scale difficulty so the learner is stretched, not overwhelmed (manage affect).

**Implication for our course:** Each lesson ends with ≥1–2 *production* exercises (speaking aloud / sentence construction), each with immediate feedback — paired with retrieval (§2) and dual-coded prompts (§6). Output is how we close the "passive comprehension, weak speaking" gap that immersion data warns about, which matters especially for a "speaking/living/working in Japan" goal.

## 6. Interleaving vs. Blocking

**The finding.** Mixing different item types/skills within a practice session (interleaving) generally beats practicing one type in a block, especially for tasks requiring *discrimination* between similar categories. Brunmair & Richter (2019) meta-analyzed 59 studies (238 effect sizes): a **medium overall interleaving advantage, Hedges' g ≈ 0.42** (95% CI [0.34, 0.50]). The benefit was largest when categories were *confusable* — visual categorization (paintings g ≈ 0.67) — and smaller for math (g ≈ 0.34) ([Brunmair & Richter 2019, *Psychological Bulletin*, Semantic Scholar entry](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Similarity-matters:-A-meta-analysis-of-interleaved-Brunmair-Richter/bb5392e8eaf53a38cc0d147f301cce74cecb4436)). "Similarity matters": interleaving helps most precisely where learners must tell similar things apart.

**Strength of evidence: strong overall, with a clear moderator (similarity).** Robust meta-analytic effect, but not universal — interleaving can hurt when items aren't confusable or when the learner needs to first build a basic schema.

**Design rule.** After an item is *introduced* in a focused block, **mix it into interleaved review** with similar-but-contrasting items — exactly the things Japanese learners confuse: particles (は/が/を/に/で), similar kana, look-alike kanji, transitive/intransitive verb pairs, counters, pitch-accent minimal pairs. Don't interleave brand-new material before a basic schema exists (introduce blocked, then interleave).

**Implication for our course:** Review sessions deliberately interleave confusable categories (particles, verb pairs, similar kanji) rather than drilling one category in isolation. Introduction can be blocked; consolidation must be interleaved.

## 7. Dual Coding (Paivio; Mayer's multimedia learning)

**The finding.** Memory is stronger when information is encoded in *both* verbal and visual channels (Paivio's dual-coding theory). Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning operationalized this: people learn better from **words + relevant pictures than words alone** — but only when the two are conceptually coherent, temporally/spatially aligned, and don't overload working memory ([Dual-coding theory — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual-coding_theory); [Clark & Paivio, *Dual coding theory and education*, PDF](https://nschwartz.yourweb.csuchico.edu/Clark%20&%20Paivio.pdf)). Success depends on the *quality of the word–image mapping*, not the mere presence of pictures — irrelevant or redundant images can backfire (multimedia overload).

**Strength of evidence: strong, but conditional.** Well-replicated across multimedia-learning studies; the boundary conditions (coherence, alignment, no overload) are as important as the main effect.

**Design rule.** Pair vocabulary and kanji with **a single, relevant, well-mapped image** (and, for grammar, a clean diagram), not decorative clip-art. Align audio narration with the visual (don't make the learner read and listen to different things simultaneously). Keep visuals minimal and on-point to avoid cognitive overload. This dovetails with the keyword method (§8) and component-kanji mnemonics (§9), which are dual-coding in action.

**Implication for our course:** Every vocab/kanji card carries one purposeful image + aligned audio; grammar uses spare diagrams. We audit images for *relevance*, not prettiness — irrelevant visuals are removed, not added.

## 8. Keyword / Mnemonic Method for Vocabulary

**The finding.** The keyword method links an L2 word to an L1 sound-alike ("keyword") plus a vivid mental image connecting keyword and meaning. It reliably **boosts initial acquisition and immediate recall** of foreign vocabulary ([keyword-method studies overview](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0959475207000357)). Long-term durability is **mixed**: some studies show faster forgetting after the initial boost, others show retention holds — and crucially, retention improves when the keyword is *supported by a picture of the keyword and referent* and by spaced retrieval rather than left as a one-off encoding trick ([Learning by the Keyword Mnemonic: Looking for Long-Term Benefits, ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/232580423_Learning_by_the_Keyword_Mnemonic_Looking_for_Long-Term_Benefits)).

**Strength of evidence: strong for initial learning; mixed for unsupported long-term retention.** The consistent message: keyword mnemonics are an *encoding accelerator*, not a *retention guarantee* — they must be combined with retrieval practice (§2) and spacing (§3).

**Design rule.** Offer keyword mnemonics (PT-BR sound-alikes are a natural fit for our learners) **as an optional encoding aid**, paired with a supporting image (dual coding, §7), and then **hand the item to the SRS** so spaced retrieval cements it. Don't present mnemonics as a substitute for review. Caveat: keyword links can fade; the SRS must keep retrieving the *actual* meaning, not just the mnemonic.

**Implication for our course:** Provide PT-BR keyword mnemonics + image for harder/abstract vocab, but always wrap them in spaced retrieval. Mnemonic = on-ramp; SRS retrieval = the road.

## 9. Component/Radical-Based Kanji Learning vs. Rote

**The finding.** Decomposing kanji into recurring components (radicals/phonetic-semantic parts) plus mnemonic stories drastically reduces memory load versus stroke-by-stroke rote, because a 10–15-stroke character becomes 2–3 known parts. L2 learners demonstrably **use semantic and phonetic radicals and component meanings** to recognize and infer unfamiliar kanji, and analytic (component-grouping) strategies outperform pure rote for retention ([Mori 1998, *Modern Language Journal*, on phonological accessibility & kanji recognition](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1540-4781.1998.tb02595.x); [Kanji learning strategies & language background, *Asian-Pacific J. of Second & Foreign Language Education* 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40862-025-00379-0); [Toyoda, *Electronic Journal of Foreign Language Teaching*](https://e-flt.nus.edu.sg/wp-content/uploads/docroot/v6n12009/toyoda.pdf)). Learners' component awareness is shaped by instruction — analytic introduction of kanji builds it. (This is dual coding + chunking + keyword mnemonics applied to orthography.) Note an L1 effect: learners from phonographic L1s (like Portuguese) benefit especially from making phonetic components explicit, since phonologically accessible characters are easier for them to remember (Mori 1998).

**Strength of evidence: moderate-to-strong and theory-consistent.** Strongly supported by chunking and dual-coding principles and by L2-kanji strategy research; fewer large RCTs directly pitting "component+mnemonic" vs. "rote" head-to-head, but the converging evidence and mechanism are solid. (Popular "cuts memorization by 80%" claims are illustrative, not measured — cite mechanism, not the slogan.)

**Design rule.** Teach kanji **bottom-up by component**: introduce high-frequency radicals/components first, then build characters as compositions of known parts with a short mnemonic story (and an image — §7). **Lead with semantic radicals** — Toyoda found learners infer meaning more reliably from semantic than phonetic components, and Japanese phonetic components are often unreliable predictors of reading. Make **phonetic components explicit as a *secondary* aid**: Mori (1998) found phonographic-L1 learners (like PT-BR speakers) encode phonologically *accessible* kanji more easily but lean harder on phonological cues and decode less flexibly — so phonetic hints help a *relative weakness*, they are not the primary lever. Sequence by frequency (§4) and reuse: each new kanji should mostly recombine already-known components. Feed every component and character into spaced retrieval (§3) — production of the reading/meaning, not just recognition (§2).

> **Verify-pass correction:** earlier framing over-weighted phonetic components; softened above to lead with
> semantic radicals + context (rated higher by Toyoda), phonetic as a secondary scaffold (Mori). Also note: the
> JLPT word-count anchors in §4 are **unofficial community estimates** (the JLPT publishes no official lists),
> and the Brunmair & Richter interleaving effect is g≈0.42 overall (treat the exact CI as unverified).

**Implication for our course:** Kanji curriculum is component-first and frequency-ordered, with mnemonic + image per character and explicit phonetic-component hints; rote stroke-drilling is minimized. Reading and meaning are both retrieval-tested in the SRS.

## 10. Pitch Accent & Prosody in Comprehensibility/Intelligibility

**The finding.** Japanese has a lexical pitch-accent system (e.g., 箸 *háshi* "chopsticks" vs. 橋 *hashí* "bridge"). Suprasegmentals — pitch accent, speech rate, rhythm — are a documented **prominent and characteristic feature of spoken Japanese and a real factor in how intelligible/acceptable learner speech sounds to natives.** In studies of L2-Japanese comprehensibility, native raters' judgments correlate significantly with surface features including pitch accent and speech rate ([Saito & Akiyama, *Linguistic Correlates of Comprehensibility in Second Language Japanese Speech*, John Benjamins](https://benjamins.com/catalog/jslp.3.2.02sai); [ResearchGate copy](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/314286587_Linguistic_Correlates_of_Comprehensibility_in_Second_Language_Japanese_Speech)). Suprasegmentals are often **harder for L2 learners to acquire than segments**, and sensitivity to Japanese pitch is additive across a learner's languages — prior tone/pitch experience helps, but PT-BR (a stress/intonation language without lexical tone) gives our learners little transfer ([SSLA, *Second and Third Language Learners' Sensitivity to Japanese Pitch Accent Is Additive*](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/studies-in-second-language-acquisition/article/abs/second-and-third-language-learners-sensitivity-to-japanese-pitch-accent-is-additive/DD55DD75A260E90EBC312A93C4ADDEBE)).

**Strength of evidence: moderate.** Clear evidence that prosody contributes to comprehensibility ratings and that perfect native-like pitch is *not* required for intelligibility; weaker/under-studied on exactly how much explicit pitch instruction at N5–N4 changes outcomes. The broader L2-pronunciation literature (Munro & Derwing) supports prioritizing *comprehensibility/intelligibility* over native-like accent.

**Design rule.** Make pitch/prosody **audible and modeled from day one** (high-quality native audio for every word and sentence; shadowing tasks), and teach pitch *patterns* explicitly where they carry contrast or are high-frequency — but **target intelligibility, not native perfection**, to avoid demotivation. Flag and drill the small set of high-frequency minimal pairs and the common accent patterns (heiban/atamadaka etc.) rather than annotating every word exhaustively at beginner level. Use interleaving (§6) for pitch minimal pairs and dual coding (§7: a pitch-contour glyph) to make accent visible.

**Implication for our course:** Audio-first with native models and shadowing in every lesson; explicit but *selective* pitch-accent instruction (high-frequency contrasts and common patterns) framed around being understood, not flawless. We accept "comprehensible accented Japanese" as the N5→N4 target and don't over-correct.

## Cross-cutting synthesis (the design backbone)

- **Engine = retrieval (§2) + spacing (§3).** These are the strongest, most replicated effects; the SRS is the spine of the course, machine-scheduled because learner metacognition is unreliable.
- **Content constraint = frequency-ordered vocab at ~95–98% coverage (§4).** Build the spaced frequency list first; everything else draws from it.
- **Encoding boosters = dual coding (§7), keyword mnemonics (§8), component kanji (§9).** These accelerate *initial* learning and must always be wrapped in retrieval+spacing — never standalone.
- **Discrimination = interleaving (§6).** Introduce blocked, consolidate interleaved, especially for confusable Japanese categories.
- **Speaking = pushed output (§5) + prosody (§10).** Required for the "live/work in Japan" goal that input-only designs underserve.
- **Krashen/Swain (§1, §5) are heuristics, not foundations.** We honor "lots of comprehensible input, low anxiety, real production" as design values, but we *build* on the measurable mechanisms.

- [Saito & Akiyama, *Linguistic Correlates of Comprehensibility in Second Language Japanese Speech* (John Benjamins)](https://benjamins.com/catalog/jslp.3.2.02sai) — pitch accent, rate, lexical variation predict native comprehensibility ratings.
- [*Second and Third Language Learners' Sensitivity to Japanese Pitch Accent Is Additive* (SSLA, Cambridge)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/studies-in-second-language-acquisition/article/abs/second-and-third-language-learners-sensitivity-to-japanese-pitch-accent-is-additive/DD55DD75A260E90EBC312A93C4ADDEBE) — pitch sensitivity additive across known languages; limited transfer for non-tone L1s.
