# The Competitive Bar for Japanese Courses in Brazil

Internal design/research note. Scope: who teaches Japanese to Brazilians today, in what format, at what price, and where the quality ceiling sits — so we can define concretely what a "best paid-grade" course (zero → N5 → N4, oriented to speaking/living/working in Japan) must beat.

---

## 1. Market map — four distinct tiers

The Brazilian Japanese-learning market is unusually segmented, and the segments barely compete with each other. Understanding the segmentation is the first strategic insight, because it means we are not entering "one market" — we are choosing which incumbent's weakness to attack.

1. **Community / cultural-association schools** (Nikkei tradition): Bunkyo, Aliança Cultural Brasil-Japão, Instituto Cultural Brasil-Japão (ICBJ), ~325 community schools nationwide, ~106 affiliated to the Centro Brasileiro de Língua Japonesa (CBLJ). In-person, semester-based, heritage-rooted.
2. **The Japan Foundation (Fundação Japão São Paulo / FJSP)** and its method ecosystem: the *Marugoto* curriculum, the free *Minato* online platform, JF Standard alignment. This is the de-facto "official" methodology that the cultural associations have largely adopted.
3. **University programs**: USP (Letras-Japonês, the oldest and only one in Latin America with classical-Japanese and a PhD program), UnB (Licenciatura, 9 semesters), UFRJ, UFRGS (extension courses + Memorial da Imigração). Academic, teacher-training-oriented, not consumer products.
4. **Paid online consumer courses** (the real commercial competitors): Instituto Kyoto, NihonGO Premium (Ricardo Cruz), Fluency Academy, "Falando Japonês do Zero" (Naoki Amorim), Japonês Descomplicado, Ninki, Danki Code, "Desvendando a Língua Japonesa", plus app-tier (Duolingo, LingoDeer, Busuu) and free creators (NHK World, Fale em Japonês).

**Implication for our course:** Our true commercial rival is tier 4 (paid online). But our *quality benchmark* should be set by the best of tiers 1–3, because that is where Brazilians who are serious about living/working in Japan currently have to go — and it is precisely those serious learners we want. We should beat tier-4 production/convenience while matching tier-1/2 rigor.

---

## 2. Paid online courses — what they cover, format, price, claims

Concrete data points gathered (prices are list/promo BRL as advertised; treat as order-of-magnitude, not quotes):

- **Instituto Kyoto** — 240+ video lessons, 70+ hours, covering N5–N2 (60 lessons each for N5/N4/N3, 30 for N2), "150-hour" certificate issued in PT + JP with a verification code. Format: lifetime-access recorded video + 1 year of live classes. Methodology pitch: visual **mnemonics** for kanji, "gamified". Price advertised ~R$598 cash (or 12× R$59.80), anchored against a R$1,200 "from" price.
- **NihonGO Premium (Ricardo Cruz)** — modular, two-track ("traditional theory" + anime/pop-culture immersion), proprietary "Nuvem de Hábitos" routine method, claims to take a dedicated student to N2. Explicitly targets the otaku/anime audience. Price typically ~R$1,997 (promos ~R$1,500); note a separate skdesu mention of an annual figure (~R$497) for a related/earlier offer — the headline product is the ~R$2k course.
- **Falando Japonês do Zero (Naoki Amorim)** — 200+ recorded lessons + PDFs + live classes, WhatsApp support, anime/series/film clips, lifetime access. Pop-culture leaning.
- **Japonês Descomplicado** — 12-week conversation focus, PT+JP workbooks per module, zero → intermediate.
- **Ninki / Danki Code** — Ninki: weekly live conversation classes, structured steps. Danki Code: only ~43 video lessons, "basic Japanese from zero" — i.e., very thin.
- **Fluency Academy** — generalist multi-language platform; markets "fluency in 12 months," subscription/annual access model.
- General price band reported for online courses: **~R$50–R$300/month**, vs **R$400+/month** for in-person — useful for grounding "paid-grade."

Recurring claims to be skeptical of (and to beat honestly): "fluency in 12 months," "equivalent to 2 years of in-person," "understand anime without subtitles," lifetime access framed as a feature when it often masks an abandoned, un-updated course.

**Implication for our course:** The paid-online ceiling is ~R$600–R$2,000 for a recorded-video product, frequently anime-themed, with thin live support and JLPT mapping that is asserted rather than demonstrated. To be "paid-grade" we should price in this band (one-time R$600–R$1,500 or a sub at R$80–R$200/mo is defensible) and out-rigor every one of them on grammar depth, pronunciation, and verifiable JLPT alignment — without leaning on the anime gimmick as the core selling point.

## 3. The Japan Foundation / Marugoto layer — the "official" method and its built-in gaps

Marugoto is the JF Standard / CEFR-based curriculum (A1–C2, mapped roughly A2 ≈ N4) adopted by Aliança Cultural, ICBJ, and FJSP itself. Aliança runs it in-person (twice-weekly regular, plus intensive that compresses one semester into a month) across São Joaquim, Pinheiros, Vergueiro, São Paulo. FJSP also offers **Marugoto Online** and the **Minato** platform with a **free Portuguese-language A1 course** (6-month window) — a genuinely strong, legitimacy-carrying free option.

The decisive finding is from FJSP's **own Marugoto FAQ**, which openly states the method's design choices:
- It **does not emphasize writing characters** and has **no in-class character-writing practice**.
- Hiragana/katakana appear in the A1 (Katsudoo) book but each kana is **not drilled** in class.
- At A1, Japanese text is printed **with romaji underneath** (e.g., こんばんは shown as "ko-n-ba-n-wa").
- Kanji are introduced gradually and **memorizing all of them is not required**; writing kanji is **not required** because, per FJSP, "what matters today is being able to type Japanese on a computer."
- The method is communicative-first: it prioritizes situational speaking over grammatical theory.

These are deliberate pedagogical positions, not accidents — but they are exactly the gaps a learner aiming to *live and work* in Japan (read signs, fill forms, pass JLPT, handle workplace text) will hit. A separately published "Marugoto Gramática" supplement exists precisely because the core books underplay explicit grammar.

**Implication for our course:** The single most authoritative, widely-adopted curriculum in Brazil (1) uses romaji crutches at A1, (2) does not teach character writing, (3) treats grammar as secondary, and (4) maps to CEFR not directly to JLPT prep. That is our clearest differentiation lane: explicit, well-sequenced grammar; kana/kanji *production* (handwriting + recognition + typing); romaji weaned off fast and deliberately; and a curriculum that is JLPT-mapped *and* survival/work-task mapped. We can position as "communicative like Marugoto, but with the literacy and grammar spine Marugoto deliberately omits."

## 4. Cultural-association / Nikkei schools — heritage strength, modern decline

Quantified picture (FJSP 2012 survey via Discover Nikkei): **~325 Japanese-language schools in Brazil**, ~20,000 students, ~1,000 teachers of whom **~85% are Nikkei**. ~106 schools affiliated to CBLJ. Historically these schools (pre-1945) existed to preserve language, culture and "moral," importing Japanese textbooks. Concentration in SP, Paraná, Mato Grosso do Sul, Pará.

Structural problems, well documented:
- **Generational erosion**: heavy reliance on heritage learners while 3rd/4th/5th-generation Nikkei increasingly do not study Japanese; the student-to-Nikkei-population ratio is described as very low. CBLJ leadership frames this as gradual loss of identity.
- **Audience shift**: a rising share of *non-Nikkei* Brazilians (anime/manga-driven), in some Northeastern schools 20–100% non-descendant — a population the heritage model was never built for and doesn't market to.
- These schools are in-person, geographically tied to Nikkei population centers, semester-paced, and historically textbook-traditional (now largely Marugoto). They have authenticity and native/heritage teachers but limited scale, modern UX, or distance reach.

**Implication for our course:** There is a large, growing, *underserved* non-Nikkei beginner audience (anime/work/travel-motivated, often outside SP/Paraná) that the heritage schools attract but are structurally unsuited to serve at scale. That is precisely our addressable market. We should borrow the heritage schools' credibility signal (native/near-native voice, cultural authenticity) while delivering it nationally, online, in pt-BR, with modern pedagogy.

## 5. University programs — the quality ceiling, and why they're not a consumer threat

- **USP (FFLCH, Letras Orientais)**: Japanese created 1963 (first in Brazil), built around three axes — Língua, Literatura, Cultura — with Língua Japonesa Moderna I–VI plus Clássica I–II; the only Latin American program with classical Japanese and a Japanese PhD program; ~160 credits for the Bachelor's; CEJAP research center + 50,000-title library. Mission: train *teachers and researchers*.
- **UnB**: Licenciatura, 9 semesters, noturno, 28 seats/semester, ~9 faculty, designed for absolute beginners → qualified teachers; explicitly notes many graduates use the degree for public-service careers even without high proficiency.
- **UFRJ** (Português-Japonês licenciatura), **UFRGS** (extension courses + free A1, Memorial da Imigração).

These set the genuine academic ceiling for grammar/linguistic rigor in Brazil, but they are degree programs (years long, geographically fixed, selective, teacher-training-focused), not products a working adult buys to reach N4 to move to Japan.

**Implication for our course:** University programs prove the *rigor* bar exists and is respected in Brazil — but they are inaccessible to our target buyer. We can credibly claim "university-grade grammar and linguistics, delivered as a practical zero→N4 product for adults" — a positioning no tier-4 competitor occupies and no university bothers to.

## 6. Free / popular resources Brazilians actually use

- **Duolingo** — dominant entry point; useful for kana drilling, weak beyond that; romaji-heavy framing for beginners.
- **NHK World "Easy Japanese" / News Web Easy** — high-quality free audio + furigana reading; reputable (public broadcaster).
- **Minato / Marugoto Online (FJSP)** — free, legitimate, pt-BR A1 — the strongest *free credible* option.
- **Tae Kim's Guide** — the canonical free grammar guide (web/PDF/app), available in PT/ES translations; respected but English-origin and dry.
- **Anki** — standard SRS for vocab/kanji among serious self-learners.
- **LingoDeer / Busuu** — app tier, structured, freemium.
- **Brazilian creators**: "Fale em Japonês" (Sarah, ~200 PT videos), "123 Japonês", Luiz Rafael, "Programa Japonês Online", "Desvendando a Língua Japonesa" — funnel free YouTube/PDF into paid launches (the "lançamento" / open-then-close-enrollment model is the dominant Brazilian sales motion).

The skdesu market overview is candid that free courses are usually loss-leaders to funnel into paid products, and that they carry "certain limitations."

**Implication for our course:** The free tier is good enough to teach kana and surface grammar, which means our paid value must live where free tools are weakest: coherent grammar progression, *pronunciation/pitch*, speaking/production practice with feedback, and a finish-line (N4 + real-life Japan tasks). We should also note the prevailing Brazilian sales model (free-content funnel → timed enrollment launches) — proven, but we can differentiate with always-open, cohort-light, evergreen access if we choose.

## 7. The common shortcomings — consolidated quality ceiling

Synthesizing across all tiers, the recurring failure modes a "best-in-Brazil" course must beat:

1. **Romaji crutch** — present even in the official Marugoto A1 (romaji under every word); rampant in apps and beginner YouTube. Few courses force an early, structured transition to kana-only.
2. **No pitch accent** — pitch accent (高低アクセント) is near-universally absent from Brazilian (and most global) courses. Research consensus: it's under-taught everywhere, materials are scarce, and untrained learners make persistent errors. Almost nobody in the Brazilian market teaches it at all — a wide-open differentiator.
3. **Weak/implicit grammar depth** — the dominant communicative method (Marugoto) deliberately subordinates grammar; pop-culture courses lean on engagement over systematic grammar. Grammar rigor lives mainly in universities, out of consumer reach.
4. **Thin literacy/production** — Marugoto explicitly drops character-writing and full-kanji memorization; many video courses are passive-watch with little active output (handwriting, speaking with correction).
5. **Asserted, not demonstrated, JLPT mapping** — courses *claim* N5/N4/N2 coverage; few show item-level alignment to the JLPT (administered in Brazil by FJSP + CBLJ; N1–N5 since the 2010 reform that added N3).
6. **Outdated / abandoned content** — "lifetime access" frequently means a frozen course; heritage schools historically textbook-traditional.
7. **Overpromising** — "fluent in 12 months," "2 years in one course," "understand anime without subtitles."
8. **Geographic + audience mismatch** — rigorous options (universities, Nikkei schools) cluster in SP/Paraná and skew heritage; the fast-growing non-Nikkei national audience is served mostly by the weakest (app/video) tier.

**Implication for our course:** Each shortcoming is a concrete feature spec. Our minimum bar to credibly call ourselves "best paid-grade in Brazil": (a) deliberate romaji → kana → kanji weaning schedule; (b) actually teach pitch accent (rare enough to be a headline differentiator); (c) explicit, university-grade grammar progression in plain pt-BR; (d) active production (handwriting drills, recorded speaking, SRS); (e) published, item-level JLPT N5→N4 mapping; (f) evergreen/updated content; (g) honest outcome framing tied to JLPT + concrete Japan-life tasks; (h) national online reach to the non-Nikkei audience the incumbents miss.

---

## 8. Defining the bar to beat (decision summary)

- **Price ceiling to respect:** paid-online incumbents sit at ~R$600 (Kyoto) to ~R$2,000 (NihonGO Premium) one-time, or in-person ~R$400+/mo; subscription apps R$50–300/mo. "Paid-grade" credibly = one-time R$600–R$1,500 or R$80–R$200/mo.
- **Rigor ceiling to match:** USP/UnB-level grammar and linguistics — but none of them ship a consumer zero→N4 product.
- **Method ceiling to surpass:** Marugoto (the standard) is communicative but romaji-dependent at A1, anti-handwriting, grammar-light, and CEFR- (not JLPT-) targeted.
- **Open lanes (our differentiators):** (1) pitch accent taught at all; (2) explicit grammar spine + plain-PT explanations; (3) real literacy production (kana/kanji writing, not just typing); (4) transparent JLPT N5→N4 item mapping; (5) Japan-life/work task orientation (forms, signs, workplace registers) beyond tourist phrases; (6) national online delivery to the underserved non-Nikkei beginner; (7) honest, measurable outcomes vs. the "fluent in 12 months" hype; (8) evergreen, maintained content.
- **Credibility signals we must acquire:** native/near-native voice and cultural authenticity (the heritage schools' moat) + JLPT alignment (Fundação Japão/CBLJ's moat) + grammar rigor (the universities' moat). Owning all three at once in one consumer product is the unoccupied position.
