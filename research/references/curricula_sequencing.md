# How the Best Japanese Curricula Sequence the N5/N4 Band — and the Scaffolding We Should Adopt

Internal design note for an original Brazilian-Portuguese zero→N5→N4 course aimed at speaking/living/working in Japan. Goal: extract the *ordering logic* and *scaffolding patterns* from the leading curricula, surface the real debates (masu vs. plain first; when katakana; when kanji; particle order), and converge on a concrete recommended sequence with chunk-size numbers.

---

## 0. The landscape at a glance

The N5/N4 band (CEFR ~A1–A2) is where almost every learner is won or lost. The materials below fall into four pedagogical families, and the family largely predicts the sequencing choices:

| Resource | Family | Plain vs. masu first | Kanji start | Chunk size (per unit) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Genki 1&2 | University classroom, integrated 4-skills | **masu first** | Lesson 3 (after kana) | ~3–5 grammar pts, ~30–45 vocab |
| Minna no Nihongo | Direct-method classroom (JP-only) | **masu first, plain very late (L20)** | separate Kanji book | ~4–6 grammar pts |
| Tobira (Beginning) | Modern classroom, can-do + content | masu first, plain early-ish | Lesson 3 | ~70 vocab, integrated grammar |
| Marugoto (JF Standard) | Can-do / communicative, JF Foundation | de-emphasizes form order entirely | minimal; romaji-heavy early | topic-based, "start anywhere" |
| Tae Kim | Self-learner grammar reference | **plain (dictionary) first** | with vocab from start | reference, not chunked |
| Imabi | Exhaustive linguistic reference | plain/structural | with vocab | very large lessons, 400+ |
| Bunpro | Grammar SRS path | mixed; だ/です→masu fast | inline furigana | ~13 grammar pts/lesson |
| WaniKani | Kanji/vocab SRS | n/a (kanji only) | from level 1 | ~30 kanji + 5–7 radicals/level |
| Try! / So-matome / Shin Kanzen | JLPT exam prep | follows JLPT, not pedagogy | assumes kana known | bite-size (So-matome) → dense (SKM) |

**Implication for our course:** No single resource is a turnkey model. The "integrated classroom" family (Genki/Tobira) gives the best *lesson architecture*; the SRS tools (Bunpro/WaniKani) give the best *retention engine* and *micro-ordering*; Marugoto gives the best *can-do framing* for a "live/work in Japan" goal; Tae Kim/Imabi give the best *conceptual scaffolding* for explaining *why*. We should compose, not copy.

## 1. Genki 1 & 2 — the integrated classroom standard

Genki is 23 lessons across two volumes: Vol. 1 = Lessons 1–12 (targets JLPT N5 / CEFR A1), Vol. 2 = Lessons 13–23 (targets N4 / A2). Roughly 1,700 vocabulary words and 317 kanji are covered, and class time is budgeted at ~200 hours total (6h conversation/grammar + 3h reading/writing per lesson). Each lesson is split into two parallel tracks: **Conversation & Grammar** and **Reading & Writing** — you do the C&G half of a lesson, then the R&W half. (Genki-Online, "A Look at GENKI 3rd Edition.")

**Ordering inside the band:**
- **Kana:** Hiragana is mastered during Lesson 1, katakana during Lesson 2. Lessons 1–2 in the C&G section use *no kanji* — Japanese is written in kana with romanized readings; from Lesson 3 the romaji disappears, which is the deliberate forcing function to make you fluent in kana by end of L2.
- **Kanji:** Starts Lesson 3 (Reading & Writing section). All kanji get hiragana readings in C&G so non-kanji learners can still follow; the R&W section withholds readings for kanji already taught.
- **Grammar/verbs:** L1 basic sentence (X は Y です, question か). L3 introduces present-tense **polite (ます) verbs** and core particles (を, で, に, へ). L4 past tense + では/じゃない. L5 adjectives (い and な). L6 **て-form** (the connective workhorse). Plain/short form appears around L8 (casual speech, ~plain past) and is used heavily from there for embedded clauses, quoting (と), etc. Honorific/passive/causative live in Vol. 2 (N4).
- **Spiral/review:** Each lesson reuses prior vocab and grammar in new dialogues; the dual C&G + R&W structure means every grammar point is re-encountered in a reading. (Genki-Online.)

**Does well:** Clean, gentle ramp; the "kana now, kanji soon, romaji gone by L3" sequence is widely imitated for good reason. Strong dialogue-first contextualization. **Does poorly:** masu-first means the plain forms underlying the whole conjugation system are taught *after* their polite derivatives, so some learners memorize ます-conjugations as opaque units before seeing the dictionary form they come from. Vocab is somewhat campus/student-life centric (less ideal for working adults).

**Implication for our course:** Adopt Genki's **kana-gating** (hiragana → katakana → romaji removed by lesson ~3) almost verbatim; it is the most battle-tested scaffold in the field. Adopt its **dual-track** idea (a "communication" strand and a "literacy/reading" strand per unit) so kanji can be optional-but-present via furigana. But reconsider masu-first (see §10) and swap the campus vocab for living/working-in-Japan vocab.

## 2. Minna no Nihongo — the direct-method workhorse

Minna no Nihongo I+II spans 50 lessons (I = 1–25 ≈ N5/early N4; II = 26–50 ≈ N4/N3). The defining trait is the **direct/audiolingual method**: the main book is monolingual Japanese, with translations/grammar notes in a *separate* companion volume, and each lesson runs a fixed drill cycle — vocabulary → sentence patterns → example sentences → Exercise A (form drills) → Exercise B (substitution) → Exercise C (mini-conversation) → listening → review. It is explicitly **spiral**: each lesson is engineered to recombine previously mastered patterns. (Minna no Nihongo grammar index, learnjp.net; Minna structure summaries.)

**The famously late plain form** — confirmed from the lesson grammar pages:
- **て-form is not introduced until Lesson 14.** Verbs are taught from their ます-form, and the three verb groups + て-form derivation are presented in L14. (learnjp.net L14.)
- **Dictionary (plain non-past) form: Lesson 18** — introduced via "V-dictionary + こと + ができます" (ability). (learnjp.net L18.)
- **ない-form ~L17, plain past (た) ~L19, full plain *style* of speech: Lesson 20** — where polite vs. plain register is finally explained as a system. (learnjp.net L20.)
- **Conditional たら: Lesson 25.**

So a Minna learner spends ~13 lessons producing only ます-Japanese before meeting the dictionary form that grammatically precedes it.

**Does well:** Enormous, rigorous drill volume; superb for classroom pattern-practice and for building automaticity. Vocabulary is adult/work-oriented (it was written for technical trainees in Japan), which fits our audience better than Genki. **Does poorly:** The monolingual main book is hostile to self-study without the translation volume; the very late plain form is the canonical example of "polite-first taken to an extreme." Visually dated, drill-heavy, light on free production.

**Implication for our course:** Steal Minna's **drill ladder** (controlled form-drill → substitution → semi-free mini-dialogue is an excellent within-lesson scaffold) and its **adult/work vocabulary register**. Do **not** copy its plain-form delay — pulling the dictionary form forward is one of our biggest opportunities to beat the incumbents.

## 3. Tobira: Beginning Japanese — the modern can-do classroom

The beginner entry of the Tobira line is 10 lessons in 3 thematic units; each unit bundles 3–4 lessons around ~70 vocabulary words/phrases and related grammar. Every lesson opens with a **"Dekiru List"** (can-do objectives), vocabulary is presented twice (visual set + plain list), and — notably — **pitch accent is marked on every new word**. Grammar is taught by breaking down a key sentence with copious examples. Romaji for hiragana words stops after Lesson 1, for katakana after Lesson 2, and **kanji study begins Lesson 3**; the book ends with all kana + 139 basic kanji. (Tofugu, "Tobira 1: Beginning Japanese.")

**Does well:** Marries Genki's clean kana/kanji gating with explicit can-do goals and heavy digital integration; pitch-accent-from-day-one is rare and valuable for a *speaking* goal. **Does poorly:** Very text-dense and "rigid college-textbook" in feel; best in a guided class, less friendly for solo absolute beginners. (Tofugu.)

**Implication for our course:** Adopt **pitch-accent marking on new vocabulary from the very first lesson** — cheap to add, disproportionately valuable for our "speak in Japan" objective, and almost no Portuguese-market course does it. Adopt the **can-do header per lesson** (it doubles as motivation and as our assessment rubric). Keep our unit grammar count near Tobira's modest level rather than Minna's firehose.

## 4. Marugoto (JF Standard) — the can-do / communicative model

Marugoto is the Japan Foundation's flagship, built on the **JF Standard** (mapped to CEFR A1–B1, etc.). Its signature is a **two-book split per level**: **Katsudoo (活動, "activities")** for people who want to *communicate immediately* in everyday situations, and **Rikai (理解, "understanding")** for the *language structures* behind those situations. Crucially, the structures in Rikai are *extracted from* the can-do model conversations in Katsudoo — i.e., **function is chosen first, and grammar is reverse-engineered to serve it**, the inverse of grammar-syllabus design. Topics recur in a spiral across levels (the 15 JF topics), and lessons are deliberately **non-progressive / "start anywhere"** so a class with rotating attendance still works. (Marugoto, "Concept and special features"; Tofugu Marugoto review.)

Because it is function-first, Marugoto **de-emphasizes the masu/plain ordering question** and leans heavily on romaji and audio early (kanji literacy is not the priority); the trade is weak systematic grammar/kanji coverage. Its content is built for **foreigners living in Japan** — apologizing by email, asking for help in an emergency, talking about where you live/shop — and deliberately omits campus tropes like "what's your major." (Tofugu Marugoto review.)

**Does well:** The clearest *purpose-built-for-living-in-Japan* content set; can-do framing makes progress legible; strong audio and culture integration; recognition-before-production input load. **Does poorly:** Thin on explicit grammar and kanji; the "start anywhere" design sacrifices the cumulative grammatical build that N5/N4 actually requires; not a path to passing JLPT on its own.

**Implication for our course:** Borrow Marugoto's **can-do situations as our unit *spine*** (especially the work/daily-life/emergency situations — they match our learner's life), and its **recognition-rich audio input**, but keep an underlying **explicit grammar+kanji progression** (Genki/Bunpro-style) running beneath the can-dos. In other words: Marugoto's *what/why*, Genki's *how-it's-ordered*.

## 5. Tae Kim's Guide — plain-form-first, "Japanese from the inside"

Tae Kim's free guide exists in two versions: a gentler **Complete Guide** and a structural **Grammar Guide** meant to supplement classes. Its thesis is explicit on its own site: traditional classes "skip straight to the polite forms without explaining where they come from," whereas the guide "starts with the most fundamental ideas and builds layer by layer." (guidetojapanese.org/learn.) The pedagogical core is **plain/dictionary form first**, because it is the root from which all conjugations (negative, past, て, conditional, polite ます, etc.) are built — so teaching ます first is teaching a derivative before its base.

**Does well:** Conceptually the cleanest model of the conjugation system; eliminates the "opaque ます-block" problem; great for understanding *why* forms exist. **Does poorly:** It is a grammar reference, not a course — no chunked lessons, no drills, no listening, light vocab, and the casual-first tone can teach register-inappropriate speech to learners who then sound too blunt with strangers.

**Implication for our course:** Adopt Tae Kim's **plain-form-as-the-root teaching of conjugation** *as the explanatory backbone*, while NOT adopting his casual-register-first social default. The synthesis (see §10): introduce plain/dictionary form **early as the conjugation engine**, but front-load ます/です as the **default register learners actually speak in** with strangers, bosses, and shopkeepers — exactly our audience's situations.

## 6. Imabi — exhaustive reference, not a syllabus

Imabi is an entirely text-based, 400+ lesson reference spanning Beginner→Veteran plus Classical and Okinawan Japanese, prized for linguistic depth and explanations not found elsewhere; it can in principle replace a textbook but has minimal exercises and no audio. (Tofugu Imabi review; AllLanguageResources.) Its ordering is structural/linguistic rather than communicative, and individual lessons are large.

**Does well:** Depth and accuracy; the best free place to resolve a subtle grammar question. **Does poorly:** Overwhelming density and lack of practice/audio make it a terrible *primary* path for a beginner.

**Implication for our course:** Use Imabi as our **internal authoring reference** for getting explanations right, and link advanced learners to it for "deep dives," but never model lesson *pacing* on it — its chunk size is the opposite of what beginners need.

## 7. Bunpro — grammar SRS with an opinionated micro-ordering

Bunpro's official N5 deck is 132 grammar items in **10 lessons (~13 items each)**, "arranged in Bunpro's custom order," with N4 similarly 10 lessons; reading practice only surfaces grammar you've already unlocked (controlled input). (Bunpro N5 deck page.) The actual N5 ordering is instructive because it is SRS-optimized rather than classroom-convenient:

- **L1 — copula + topic foundation:** だ, です, は, も, これ/それ/あれ, い-adj 良い, の (possession), い-/な-adjective concept, か (question).
- **L2 — verbs enter:** が, よ, ね, **る-verb / う-verb dictionary forms**, を, **ます**, negative verbs, と, ここ/そこ/あそこ. (Note: dictionary form and ます are introduced *together* in L2 — plain root and polite ending side by side, not 13 lessons apart as in Minna.)
- **L3 — remaining core particles:** で, に, か(or), と(with), でしょう/だろう, ある/いる, この/その/あの, んです.
- **L4 — verb forms + nominalization:** のは/のが, adj+noun, 行く, する, 来る, past plain verbs, 好き/嫌い, くない.
- **L5 — linking:** て-conjunction, ので/から (because), けど/が (but), ている.
- **L6+ — gap-filling, more linking, question words, register (じゃない/だった), prohibitive な, relative clauses.**
- **L7–10 — てから, てもいい, たい, だけ; amounts/comparatives (より, 一番, すぎる); requests/suggestions (ましょう, ませんか, てください, なくては…); giving/receiving + experience (たことがある).**

**Does well:** Demonstrates that **dictionary form and ます can coexist from the second lesson**, that **particles can be front-loaded in the first three lessons**, and that grammar can be ordered by *dependency* (you can't teach て-clauses before て-form, comparatives before adjectives). Excellent retention via SRS. **Does poorly:** It is grammar-only — no vocab teaching, no real speaking/listening, and the dense 13-item lessons assume an external course for context.

**Implication for our course:** Bunpro's N5 dependency graph is the **single best published micro-ordering** to model our grammar spine on (with our own pacing). Adopt its principle of **introducing dictionary form alongside ます early**, and its **front-loaded particle cluster (は, も, を, が, で, に, へ, と, から/まで) across the first ~3 units**. Adopt an **SRS review layer** behind every grammar point.

## 8. WaniKani — radical → kanji → vocabulary, SRS-driven

WaniKani teaches ~2,000 kanji + ~6,000 vocab across **60 levels**, each level introducing ~5–7 **radicals**, then ~30 **kanji** built from those radicals, then the **vocabulary** that uses those kanji — a strict bottom-up dependency chain glued together by **mnemonics** and **SRS** (Apprentice→Guru→Master→Enlightened→Burned, with intervals stepping 4h, 8h, ~1d, ~2d, 1w, 2w, 1mo, 4mo). You must reach a pass threshold on a level's kanji before its vocab unlocks. (WaniKani; Tofugu "Learn Kanji with Radicals and Mnemonics.") Full completion is ~1–2 years.

The deeper pedagogical claim (Tofugu's radicals guide): kanji are not memorized as strokes but as **compositions of named radicals + a mnemonic story**, which is what produces the reported 5–10× speedup. (Tofugu radicals guide.)

**Does well:** The **radical→kanji→vocab dependency order** and **mnemonic+SRS** combination is the most effective mass-kanji method in wide use; it makes new kanji feel like recombinations of known parts rather than novel shapes. **Does poorly:** Pure recognition (reading) focus — almost no handwriting, no grammar, no listening; rigid ordering can teach kanji long before you'd meet the word in context; the first ~10 levels feel slow.

**Implication for our course:** Adopt the **radical→kanji→vocabulary build order and SRS scheduling** for our literacy strand, and the **mnemonic-per-kanji** technique (adapted into Portuguese — mnemonics must be authored in pt-BR, not translated from English, or they stop working). But **bind kanji to vocabulary the learner is actually meeting in the grammar/communication strand**, rather than WaniKani's standalone frequency order, so reading reinforces speaking.

## 9. JLPT prep series (Try!, Nihongo So-matome, Shin Kanzen Master)

These are **review/exam** materials, not first-contact courses; their ordering follows the JLPT specification, not a pedagogical build.
- **Try!** integrates each grammar point into a short reading/listening context — "see the grammar used, then drill it" — making it the most *communicative* of the three and a reasonable light primary-adjacent text. (Try! N5 product pages.)
- **Nihongo So-matome** is deliberately **bite-sized**: organized into small daily portions over a fixed number of weeks (e.g., a few items/day with immediate varied exercises) — optimized for steady, low-friction review. (So-matome vs SKM comparisons.)
- **Shin Kanzen Master** is **comprehensive and demanding** — more content, deeper, harder, the choice "if you want to pass no matter what," but heavy and less gentle. (insidethatjapanesebook; notes.inhae.blog.)

**Does well:** So-matome's **daily micro-chunk cadence** is a great model for habit formation; SKM's **rigor** is the model for exhaustive coverage; Try!'s **context-embedded grammar** is the model for not teaching grammar in a vacuum. **Does poorly:** None of them teaches from zero or sequences pedagogically — they assume kana known and a base course done.

**Implication for our course:** Use these as the **N5/N4 coverage checklist and as the model for two distinct review modes**: a So-matome-style **daily micro-review** (a few items, fast) layered on top of a Try!-style **context-embedded** presentation, with an SKM-style **comprehensive end-of-band review** before each JLPT. Map our grammar spine to the published JLPT N5/N4 lists so nothing is missed.

## 10. The two big debates, decided

### 10a. masu-form first vs. plain-form first
- **masu-first (Genki, Minna, most classrooms):** rationale is social safety — learners speak politely to strangers immediately and never sound rude; ます is also trivially regular to inflect (ません / ました / ませんでした). The cost, taken to the extreme in **Minna (plain not until L18–20)**, is that learners drill polite *derivatives* for many weeks before meeting the dictionary-form *base* they're derived from. (Smile Nihongo; learnjp.net L14/L18/L20.)
- **plain-first (Tae Kim, many self-learners):** rationale is structural — the dictionary form is the root of all conjugation, including ます itself, and it's what you actually hear in casual speech, anime, and read in writing. The cost is social: casual-first learners can sound blunt/childish to strangers and bosses. (guidetojapanese.org; JapaneseAmmo's well-known argument.)
- **Evidence quality:** This is a long-running *pedagogical-opinion* debate, not a settled empirical one — strong rationales on both sides, little controlled-study data. **Bunpro's middle path is the strongest existing compromise:** introduce **dictionary form and ます in the same early lesson** (its L2), teach the *register meaning* of polite vs. plain explicitly and early, but make **ます the default production register** because that is the register our learners need for shops, offices, and officials in Japan.

**Decision for our course:** **Register-explicit, ます-default, plain-form-early.** From the very first verb lesson, show both the dictionary form (as the conjugation engine and the "dictionary lookup" form) *and* the ます form (as the polite default they'll speak). Teach the politeness distinction as a first-class concept, not a late reveal. This beats Minna's 18-lesson delay and Tae Kim's social bluntness simultaneously.

### 10b. recognition vs. production balance
Marugoto/Try! lean recognition-rich (lots of audio/reading input before output); Minna leans production-drill; WaniKani/Bunpro are recognition SRS. **Decision:** front-load **recognition (listening + reading input)** per new item, then a **Minna-style controlled→semi-free production ladder**, then a **can-do free-production task** (Marugoto-style). Recognition first, production close behind, within the same unit.

## 11. Cross-cutting recommended sequencing for N5/N4

A composed order, justified by the dependency logic the resources collectively reveal:

1. **Pre-unit — Script gate (Genki/Tobira model).** Hiragana first (mastered before katakana), then katakana, with romaji as a temporary crutch removed by ~unit 3. **Introduce katakana right after hiragana, not later** — our learners hit katakana constantly (foreign words, brand/place names, menus, their own foreign name) from day one in Japan; deferring it (some courses push it weeks out) is a mistake for a *living-in-Japan* audience.
2. **Unit 1 — Copula + topic + existence.** X は Y です / ですか, これ/それ/あれ, の (possession), か, も. Particle cluster begins: **は, も, の**.
3. **Unit 2 — Verbs enter, both forms together.** Dictionary form **and** ます introduced side by side; register (polite vs plain) named explicitly; particles **を, が**; basic SOV word order.
4. **Unit 3 — Place/time/direction particles.** **で, に, へ, と, から/まで**; this completes the "8 particles that cover ~90% of usage." Negative and past polite verbs.
5. **Unit 4 — Adjectives.** い- and な-adjectives, present/past, affirmative/negative; adjective + noun; basic comparison teaser.
6. **Unit 5 — て-form (the hinge).** Connecting actions, requests (てください), ている (progressive/state), permission (てもいい). て-form is the gateway to half of N4, so it should land mid-N5, **not** at Minna's Lesson 14.
7. **Units 6–8 (late N5) — desire/ability/nominalization.** たい, こと/の nominalization, ことができる (ability), comparatives (より / 一番), question words (誰/どこ/いつ/なぜ), reasons (から/ので), quoting (と思う / と言う).
8. **N4 band (Units 9+) — plain-form-dependent grammar.** Plain past for relative clauses, casual speech style, たら/ば/と/なら conditionals, potential form, volitional, transitive/intransitive pairs, giving/receiving (あげる/くれる/もらう), たことがある, ようになる, passive, then causative and causative-passive, keigo basics last.

Kanji runs as a **parallel literacy strand** from unit 3 onward (radical→kanji→vocab, SRS, mnemonics in pt-BR), with kanji chosen to match vocabulary already met in the communication strand. Pitch accent is marked on all new vocab from unit 1.

**Implication for our course:** This order is *dependency-correct* (no て-clauses before て-form, no relative clauses before plain past, no comparatives before adjectives), *register-honest* (politeness taught early), and *purpose-fit* (katakana and work/daily vocab front-loaded for life in Japan).

## 12. Concrete chunk-size guidance (items per lesson)

Synthesizing the observed sizes — Tobira ~70 vocab/lesson, Genki ~30–45 vocab + 3–5 grammar, Bunpro ~13 grammar/lesson, WaniKani ~30 kanji + 5–7 radicals/level, So-matome a few items/day:

- **New grammar points per lesson: 3–5.** (Genki's range; Bunpro's 13 is too dense without a teacher and is SRS-paced, not first-contact-paced.)
- **New vocabulary per lesson: 15–25** for a self-study cadence (below Tobira's classroom 70, which assumes instructor pacing). Cap closer to 15 for the first few units.
- **New kanji per lesson: 5–10**, introduced via 3–6 radicals first (WaniKani's ratio scaled down for a slower beginner ramp).
- **Daily micro-review: ~5–15 SRS items** (So-matome-style cadence) drawn from the spaced queue, distinct from new-lesson load.
- **Within-lesson scaffold (the Minna ladder, modernized):** recognition input (audio + reading of the target in context) → form/pattern explanation (Tae-Kim-style "why") → controlled drill → substitution drill → semi-free mini-dialogue → one **can-do task** (Marugoto-style) that is the lesson's success criterion.
- **Spiral:** every new lesson must re-surface vocab/grammar from the previous 2–3 lessons in its examples and tasks, and the SRS layer guarantees long-tail review (WaniKani/Bunpro intervals: ~4h → 1d → 3d → 1w → 2w → 1mo → 4mo).

**Implication for our course:** Target a **"3–5 grammar / 15–25 vocab / 5–10 kanji"** lesson, wrapped in the recognition→drill→production→can-do ladder, with an always-on SRS review queue. This is dense enough to progress at a real N5→N4 pace yet light enough for unaccompanied adult self-study — the gap most pt-BR-market courses fail to hit.
