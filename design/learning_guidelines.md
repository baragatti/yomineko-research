## A. The SLA engine

### Retrieval (testing effect)
- DO put в‰Ґ1 active-recall and в‰Ґ1 production exercise in every content lesson (retrieval beats restudy, gв‰€+0.51; vs filler gв‰€+0.93 вЂ” Adesope 2017).
- DO design the FIRST retrieval of any item to succeed (~70%+): only successful first retrieval yields long-term gains; failed retrieval в‰€ restudy (Karpicke).
- DO give immediate corrective feedback on every recall attempt (feedback is what rescues low-success retrievals вЂ” Karpicke; spec В§1.2).
- DON'T rely on rereading / re-recognition to teach (passive rereading creates an illusion of learning and overconfidence вЂ” Roediger).
- DON'T read "MC > short-answer on average" as "prefer multiple-choice" (the MC edge is format-match + feedback dependent; production transfers better вЂ” Adesope 2017). Keep the mix.

### Spacing
- DO schedule each item on an expanding lag across lessons (introduce в†’ ~D1 в†’ ~D3 в†’ ~D7 в†’ вЂ¦): spaced beats massed (Kim & Webb 2022).
- DO size the first gap as a PROPORTION of the target horizon that SHRINKS as the horizon grows (~20вЂ“40% for a 1-week goal, ~5вЂ“10% for a 1-year goal вЂ” Cepeda 2008).
- DO ensure massed first exposure inside a lesson is followed by spaced review later (massed gives high initial success but poor retention вЂ” Cepeda).
- DON'T bunch all reviews of an item into one lesson and never revisit it (no spacing = no durable retention).

### Coverage (vocabulary selection)
- DO author each controlled text so known-token coverage is ~100% within the lesson's cumulative introduced set (i+1; the 95/98% literature is for UNASSISTED authentic reading, not beginner texts вЂ” Laufer & Ravenhorst-Kalovski).
- DO use the 95вЂ“98% / 4вЂ“5kвЂ“8k-family findings only to justify frequency-ordered SELECTION, never as a per-lesson coverage target (category error if applied to a zeroв†’N4 course).
- DON'T introduce a token in a sentence that the learner has not met, except the lesson's single i+1 item (uncontrolled coverage tanks comprehension).

### Output (pushed production)
- DO include в‰Ґ1 sentence-construction / speaking task with an answer key per content lesson (comprehensible input is necessary but NOT sufficient; immersion leaves persistent production errors вЂ” Swain).
- DO use task repetition (say it, then say it again) for fluency (large speech-rate gains across the first 3вЂ“5 immediate repetitions вЂ” Sato 2023).

### Interleaving
- DO introduce a set BLOCKED, then interleave only CONFUSABLE/high-similarity items afterward (гЃЇ/гЃЊ, гЃ«/гЃ§, transitive/intransitive, look-alike kana/kanji, length minimal pairs) вЂ” interleaving's benefit rises with between-category similarity (Brunmair & Richter 2019, gв‰€0.42 overall).
- DON'T interleave UNRELATED new vocabulary lists (no benefit for heterogeneous word lists, g=-0.39 and non-significant; driven by low item similarity, not "vocab is special").
- DO treat visually confusable kana/kanji (дєє/е…Ґ, е¤§/зЉ¬, г‚Џ/г‚Њ/гЃ­, г‚·/гѓ„) as the VISUAL-category case where interleaving wins (paintings gв‰€0.67), after a blocked introduction of each shape.

### Dual coding & lesson load
- DO pair a new word/kanji with ONE relevant image (word+relevant image beats text alone вЂ” Mayer multimedia principle).
- DO pair graphics with spoken narration rather than on-screen text when audio exists (modality principle).
- DON'T add decorative/clip-art visuals or redundant simultaneous text channels (irrelevant visuals and redundant text raise cognitive load вЂ” redundancy effect; Sweller/Mayer).
- DO cap new load per lesson (~3вЂ“5 grammar / ~15вЂ“25 vocab / ~5вЂ“10 kanji; ~4вЂ“6 exercises; ~2.0k pt-BR chars) вЂ” element-interactivity / working-memory limits (cognitive load theory).

### Mnemonics
- DO use keyword/pt-BR mnemonics as an ENCODING ON-RAMP only, always wrapped in spaced retrieval (keyword learners can forget FASTER than rote at delay вЂ” up to ~2Г— items lost вЂ” unless wrapped in spaced recall).
- DON'T treat a mnemonic as the learning event (picture+sound kana mnemonics helped only some learners on immediate recall and NEITHER group on delayed recall).

### Component-kanji
- DO teach kanji component/radical-first, leading with the SEMANTIC radical, recombining already-known parts (analytic study beats pure rote or pure mnemonics вЂ” Flores d'Arcais).
- DO surface the PHONETIC component as a secondary reading aid where it is reliable (learners benefit from both semantic and phonetic functional components).
- DON'T order the kanji syllabus by a single raw frequency list (rankings disagree sharply across corpora вЂ” дєє is #1 in Aozora, #7 in Wikipedia; a component-based order retains better for learners outside Japan).
- DON'T lean on radical analysis alone at advanced levels (benefit shrinks with orthography-semantics inconsistency; supplement, don't replace).

### Pitch & pronunciation
- DO teach pronunciation explicitly, targeting SPECIFIC features with BOTH articulatory ("how to produce") and auditory ("how to hear") information (large effect, dв‰€0.80вЂ“0.89 вЂ” Lee, Jang & Plonsky 2015; gains strongest on controlled tasks вЂ” Saito & Plonsky 2019).
- DO train perception with HVPT-style drills: multiple speakers, varied contexts, forced-choice minimal-pair ID with trial-by-trial feedback (medium-large, durable, partly generalizable perception gains across ~79 studies).
- DO teach pitch as a LISTENING/minimal-pair skill first, with a VISUAL pitch-HEIGHT notation aligned to audio (height notation generalized to untrained words; flat notation and hand-gesture did not вЂ” 2024 study). гЃЇгЃ— (hashГ­, ponte) vs гЃЇгЃ— (hГЎshi, palitos).
- DO target intelligibility, not native perfection, and teach pitch SELECTIVELY (high-frequency minimal pairs); state the course teaches Tokyo accent and that Kansai/regional differs (avoid over-generalizing pairs).
- DESIGN CHOICE (heuristic, not a research finding): aim ~80% discrimination before pushing production of a contrast.

### Motivation & affect
- DO design for AUTONOMOUS motivation via autonomy + competence + relatedness: choice, attainable next steps, meaning ("para morar/trabalhar no JapГЈo") (autonomous motivation rв‰€.23 with achievement вЂ” SDT-L2 2025).
- DON'T over-rely on controlled motivators (streaks/points as the core driver): controlled motivation is weakly negative-to-null with achievement (scale-dependent).
- DO message "intelligibility, not perfection" explicitly to defuse pronunciation/pitch perfectionism (foreign-language anxiety suppresses output; acute for solo self-learners).

## B. Japanese-specific sequencing
- DO teach hiragana to mastery FIRST, then katakana on a need-to-read basis, with kanji introduced alongside (kana acquired in parallel in L1 schooling; a strict "all katakana before any kanji" gate is not motivated).
- DO read from kana, not romaji, from the start; wean romaji by ~unit 3 (kana is read via a sublexical phonology route; sustained romaji reliance is a practitioner-consensus crutch to drop early).
